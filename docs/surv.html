Surveillance Capitalism

What is surveillance capitalism?

Surveillance capitalism is a market-driven mechanism in which your personal data is the commodity for sale, and the capture and production of this data is based on mass internet surveillance. Companies that provide us with free internet services, such as search engines (Google) and social media sites, are often involved in this activity.

The digital revolution's ongoing advancements can be breathtaking. However, Shoshana Zuboff, a Harvard Business School professor emerita, warns that the lights, bells, and whistles have blinded us to the ways high-tech behemoths manipulate our personal data for their own gain.

These businesses capture and analyze our online activities (likes, dislikes, searches, social media, and purchases) in order to generate data that can be used for commercial purposes. And it's frequently achieved without our knowledge of the full scope of the monitoring.

Why should you worry about it?

One of Zuboff's most important lessons is that we must be mindful of what is going on and not be complacent about the surveillance we are subjected to. It's difficult to say how this will affect or alter how we interact with technology.

Many of us are aware that our personal information is being collected without our consent and used to try to persuade us to become more predictable and trustworthy customers.

Zuboff is pushing us toward this degree of consciousness, but it is up to each of us to make the requisite adjustments in our lives to match our behaviors with that awareness. It's extremely difficult. It's likely to be particularly challenging for digital natives, who have grown up with Facebook, Instagram, and other social media platforms.
